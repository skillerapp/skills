# Stakeholders: mapping, sequencing, escalation, and channels

Read this when the user needs to decide who to tell, in what order, through what channel, and who decides what.

## Stakeholder map

List every audience, then plot each on two axes: **harm/impact** (how affected are they) and **influence** (how much they shape the outcome). Prioritize by harm first, influence second.

Common business audiences: directly-affected individuals (injured customers, breach victims), employees, regulators/authorities, business partners and suppliers, broad customer base, investors/shareholders, media, the wider public, and online communities.

For each, capture: what they need to know, what they'll fear, what action you want from them, who owns the relationship, and the channel.

## Sequencing rule

**No high-priority stakeholder should learn about the crisis from the media.** Default sequence:

1. People directly harmed (individual, direct, often before any public statement).
2. Employees (they are your front line and they will talk — brief before they read it externally).
3. Regulators / authorities where obligations exist (timing may be legally mandated — see `regulatory-clocks.md`).
4. Key partners / suppliers with operational dependency.
5. Broad customer base.
6. Media / public (often simultaneous with #5 via owned channel).
7. Investors (timed to securities-disclosure requirements; coordinate with legal — do not selectively disclose).

Speed compresses this in fast-moving breaks, but order of *priority* holds even when timing collapses.

## Channel matrix

Match audience to channel; route all of them back to one authoritative owned source.

| Audience | Primary channel | Owned source to point to |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Directly harmed | Direct (call, email, letter) | Dedicated support line/page |
| Employees | Internal (all-hands, email, intranet) | Internal FAQ |
| Regulators | Formal filing / direct contact | — |
| Customers | Email, in-product, status page | Status page / incident page |
| Media | Press release, spokesperson | Newsroom / press page |
| Public / social | Owned social, website | Pinned post linking incident page |
| Investors | Regulated disclosure (e.g., filings) | IR page |

Stand up the **single owned channel** (status page, incident page, pinned post) early — it's the URL every other message links to and the place you control entirely.

## Escalation protocol

Define the trigger and path before the crisis, then use the level set in Phase 0:

- **Level 1:** comms lead handles with pre-approved response; logs it; informs manager.
- **Level 2:** activate core crisis team within a set window; notify senior leadership; designate incident commander + spokesperson.
- **Level 3:** full team + executive/board engagement; CEO briefed and possibly fronting; legal and regulatory tracks running; war-room cadence established.

Specify who has authority to: declare a crisis, approve external statements, speak to media, and stand the team down. Ambiguity here costs hours you don't have.

## Crisis team RACI

Assign clearly so nothing is dropped and no one freelances:

| Function | Typical owner |
| --- | --- |
| Incident commander (decisions, coordination) | Crisis lead / COO |
| Spokesperson (the one public voice) | Designated exec / head of comms |
| Fact log owner (single source of truth) | Comms ops / chief of staff |
| Legal & regulatory | General counsel |
| Operational fix | Relevant function head (IT, ops, product) |
| Employee comms | HR + internal comms |
| Monitoring & social | Comms/social team |
| Executive/board liaison | CEO office |

One spokesperson, one fact log, one incident commander — singular by design.
