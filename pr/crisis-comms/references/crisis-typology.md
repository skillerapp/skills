# Crisis typology: families, clusters, and tactical playbooks

Read this to map a specific business crisis to its response posture and to get family-specific tactics. Pair it with `statements.md` for the wording and `regulatory-clocks.md` when disclosure timing is in play.

## How to classify

Place the crisis in a cluster by how much responsibility stakeholders will *attribute* to the organization (perception drives the response, not internal belief in your own innocence):

- **Victim** — low attributed responsibility (sabotage, tampering, false rumor, natural disaster). → Inform, reassure, correct falsehoods.
- **Accidental** — moderate (technical failure, unforeseen accident, no intent). → Diminish honestly + corrective action.
- **Preventable** — high (human error, misconduct, knowing violation, negligence). → Rebuild: apologize, own it, remediate.

When in doubt about cluster, assume the *higher* responsibility level publicly — under-owning is the costlier error.

## Operational crises

### Data breach / cyber incident
- Cluster: usually accidental → preventable (depends on whether basic safeguards failed).
- First moves: contain, engage forensics and legal, open the fact log, **assume disclosure clocks are running** (see `regulatory-clocks.md`).
- Message priorities: what data, whose, what window, what risk, what to do now (passwords, monitoring), what you're offering (e.g., credit monitoring).
- Pitfalls: announcing scope before forensics confirm it (you'll revise upward — worst look); minimizing ("no evidence of misuse" ages badly).

### Product recall / safety issue
- Cluster: accidental → preventable.
- First moves: stop distribution, coordinate with regulators, prioritize physical safety messaging above all.
- Message priorities: who is at risk, exactly which products (lot numbers/serials), what harm, how to return/stop using, refund/replacement path.
- Pitfalls: legal-driven hedging that obscures the safety instruction; slow recall that looks like profit-over-safety.

### Service outage / disruption
- Cluster: usually accidental.
- First moves: status page as single source of truth, frequent cadence even without resolution.
- Message priorities: what's down, who's affected, ETA or next-update time, workaround, post-mortem commitment.
- Pitfalls: vague "some users may experience issues" when it's a full outage; going quiet between updates.

## Financial / legal / regulatory crises

### Financial restatement / earnings shock / fraud allegation
- Cluster: accidental → preventable.
- First moves: **legal and securities counsel lead**; comms is tightly coordinated with disclosure obligations; investor relations aligned.
- Message priorities: precise, lawyer-approved facts; avoid forward-looking promises; treat all audiences as if every word is on the record and material.
- Pitfalls: selective disclosure (fair-disclosure rules); reassurance that outpaces verified facts; tone that ignores employees/customers while courting investors.

### Lawsuit / regulatory investigation / enforcement action
- Cluster: contested — often you cannot admit or deny.
- First moves: counsel sets the boundary of what can be said; build messages that show respect for the process without conceding liability.
- Message priorities: confirm awareness, state cooperation/commitment to process, decline to litigate in public — but never "no comment" cold.
- Pitfalls: defensive combativeness that reads as guilt; commentary that prejudices the case; contradicting the legal filing.

> For all of this family: comms does not run point — legal does. Always recommend counsel review before release and note you are not a lawyer.

## Reputational / misconduct / leadership crises

### Executive misconduct / leadership scandal
- Cluster: preventable.
- First moves: separate the institution from the individual quickly if warranted; independent investigation signals seriousness; board may need its own voice.
- Message priorities: clear values statement, concrete action on the individual, protection for any victims, what changes structurally.
- Pitfalls: protecting the executive over stakeholders (the second-day story); vague "personnel matter" language when accountability is expected.

### Social-media backlash / viral controversy
- Cluster: ranges victim (false rumor) to preventable (a real offense).
- First moves: assess validity fast before responding; speed matters but a wrong response pours fuel on it.
- Message priorities: if you erred, own it plainly and early; if it's misinformation, correct with evidence calmly and once-firmly (don't feed a pile-on).
- Pitfalls: defensive or sarcastic tone; deleting/hiding (screenshots are forever); over-responding to a story that would have died.

### Layoffs / restructuring
- Cluster: preventable in perception (a company choice), even when economically forced.
- First moves: tell affected employees first and directly — never let them learn from a press leak or a Slack channel.
- Message priorities: respect and dignity, honest rationale, support offered (severance, services), what it means for those who remain.
- Pitfalls: euphemism ("rightsizing"), celebratory framing, leaks from poor sequencing.

## Quick cluster reference table

| Crisis | Likely cluster | Lead posture |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Data breach | Accidental→Preventable | Diminish→Rebuild + protect users |
| Product recall | Accidental→Preventable | Safety-first + remediate |
| Outage | Accidental | Inform + cadence |
| Restatement / fraud | Accidental→Preventable | Counsel-led, precise |
| Lawsuit / investigation | Contested | Cooperate, don't concede |
| Exec misconduct | Preventable | Rebuild + structural change |
| Viral backlash | Victim→Preventable | Validate first, then match |
| Layoffs | Preventable (perceived) | Dignity + honesty |
