# Internal comms, executive/board briefings, and spokesperson prep

Read this when the audience is inside the organization or when you're preparing the person who will speak.

## Contents
- Employee communications
- Executive / board briefings
- Spokesperson preparation

## Employee communications

Employees are simultaneously an audience, your front line, and a leak risk. Brief them *before or in lockstep with* the external announcement.

Structure:
1. What happened (the same verified facts as external, no spin internally — they'll spot it).
2. What it means for them and their work.
3. What the company is doing.
4. What you need from them — critically, **what to do if contacted** (route media/customer questions to comms; don't post personally).
5. Where to get updates and ask questions (internal FAQ, channel, point of contact).

Tone: candid and respectful. Treating employees as insiders earns their discretion; treating them as a PR risk guarantees leaks. Give them a way to ask questions safely.

## Executive / board briefings

Leaders need decision-ready briefs, not narrative. Keep it to one page where possible:

```
SITUATION: what happened, current status, severity level.
IMPACT: stakeholders affected, scale, legal/regulatory exposure.
ACTIONS TAKEN: what's done and underway, by whom.
DECISIONS NEEDED: the specific calls only leadership can make (with a recommendation).
COMMS POSTURE: current public position + next planned move.
RISKS / UNKNOWNS: open questions and the worst plausible turn.
NEXT UPDATE: when leadership will hear more.
```

The board's distinct needs: governance and fiduciary exposure, regulatory/legal risk, whether the response itself is adequate, and whether the CEO should be the public face. Give them the unvarnished version — sanitized briefings to a board are their own scandal.

## Spokesperson preparation

The spokesperson is the single public voice. Prepare them before every interaction:

- **Arm with the message house** (`statements.md`): roof, pillars, proof points. They speak only from confirmed facts.
- **Drill the hard questions** — run the anticipated-Q&A list out loud; practice bridging from a hostile question to a pillar without repeating the hostile premise.
- **The discipline of "I don't know yet."** Teach them to say it plainly and pair it with what *is* known and a commitment to follow up. Speculation is the enemy.
- **Tone calibration** — empathy first, no defensiveness, no jargon, no jokes. Match gravity to the harm.
- **Non-verbals** — for broadcast, steady pace, no fidgeting, no over-blinking; for written, no exclamation points, no corporate cheer.
- **Bridging phrases** — "What I can tell you is…", "The most important thing right now is…", "Let me be clear about what we're doing…".
- **Hard rules** — never lie, never say "no comment" cold, never go off the record assuming it's safe, never speculate on cause or numbers, never disparage anyone.

Re-brief before each appearance as facts evolve — yesterday's approved line may now be wrong.
