# Regulatory and legal disclosure clocks

Read this whenever a crisis may trigger a mandatory notification or disclosure obligation — breaches, financial events, safety issues, anything regulated.

## The one rule that matters most

**Disclosure obligations vary by jurisdiction, sector, and the specific facts, and they change over time. Do not treat any deadline below as authoritative or current.** Use this file to recognize *that a clock may be running* and to prompt the right action: bring in legal/compliance counsel immediately and confirm the rules that actually apply. In a crisis, the comms timeline must be coordinated with — and often subordinate to — the legal timeline. A statement that satisfies the public but violates a disclosure rule (or vice versa) creates a second crisis.

## How to use this in a response

1. The moment a crisis could be regulated, flag it: "This may trigger mandatory reporting obligations — confirm with legal/compliance before any external statement."
2. Identify which obligations *might* apply based on what's affected (personal data? securities? consumer safety? health? finances?).
3. Coordinate sequencing so required notifications to authorities happen in the legally correct order and timing relative to public statements.
4. Never let a public statement get ahead of, contradict, or undercut a required regulatory filing.
5. State plainly that you are not a lawyer and counsel must verify and own the disclosure timeline.

## Illustrative anchors (verify currency and applicability — do not rely on these as stated)

These are commonly-cited examples to build awareness of the *types* of clocks that exist. They are not legal advice and may be outdated or inapplicable to a given situation.

- **Personal-data breaches (EU/UK GDPR):** notification to the relevant supervisory authority is generally required without undue delay and, where feasible, not later than 72 hours after becoming aware; affected individuals may also need to be told when risk is high.
- **US public-company cybersecurity incidents (SEC):** material incidents are generally disclosed on Form 8-K (Item 1.05) within four business days of determining materiality, with a narrow national-security delay mechanism.
- **US state data-breach laws:** most states require notification to affected residents (and sometimes attorneys general) within defined windows that vary by state.
- **Sector-specific regimes** (health data, financial services, critical infrastructure, telecoms, etc.) and **non-US jurisdictions** each carry their own notification triggers and timeframes.

The point of this list is not the specific numbers — it is to make the response team ask "what clocks are running here?" early, and route that question to counsel.

## Coordination checklist with legal

- Has counsel confirmed which obligations apply and their deadlines?
- Who owns each required filing/notification, and by when?
- Does the public statement align with (not contradict) the regulatory filing?
- Is the notification sequence (authorities vs. individuals vs. public) legally correct?
- Has legal reviewed the external wording for liability before release?
