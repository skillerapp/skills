# Full crisis communication plan / playbook

Read this when the user wants a complete plan or playbook (preparedness), not a single in-the-moment artifact. Pair with `assets/crisis-comms-plan-template.md` for a fillable skeleton.

A crisis communication plan is built *before* the crisis. It exists so that when something happens, the team executes a rehearsed process instead of improvising under pressure. A plan no one has read or drilled is decoration — build in the activation and rehearsal mechanics, not just the content.

## Structure

1. **Purpose and scope** — what this plan covers, what it doesn't, and how it relates to the broader business-continuity / incident-response plans.

2. **Crisis definition and severity levels** — the Phase 0 triage criteria and the Level 1/2/3 definitions, so anyone can classify an incident consistently.

3. **Activation triggers and protocol** — what conditions activate the plan, who can declare a crisis, and the first 60-minute checklist (the golden-hour playbook from Phase 1).

4. **Crisis team and RACI** — named roles (incident commander, spokesperson, fact-log owner, legal, ops, HR, board liaison) with primary and backup holders and 24/7 contact details. (See `stakeholders.md`.)

5. **Stakeholder map and notification sequence** — pre-identified audiences, priority order, owners, and channels. (See `stakeholders.md`.)

6. **Channel and asset inventory** — owned channels (status page, newsroom, social), access credentials owners, and pre-built assets (dark/standby site, holding-statement templates by scenario).

7. **Message frameworks** — the message-house method and pre-drafted holding-statement templates for the organization's most likely scenarios (breach, outage, recall, exec issue, etc.). (See `statements.md` and `crisis-typology.md`.)

8. **Scenario playbooks** — for each high-likelihood / high-impact scenario, a tailored mini-playbook: first moves, cluster/posture, key messages, stakeholders, regulatory clocks.

9. **Monitoring and escalation** — how the organization detects and tracks an emerging crisis, and the cadence for sustained response.

10. **Regulatory and legal coordination** — disclosure obligations to be aware of and the legal-review gate before release. (See `regulatory-clocks.md`; verify current rules with counsel.)

11. **Recovery and post-incident review** — the rebuild approach and the review template that feeds lessons back into the plan.

12. **Maintenance and rehearsal** — review cadence, tabletop-simulation schedule, and contact-list refresh. **A plan is only as good as its last drill.**

## Risk assessment to drive the scenario playbooks

Help the user prioritize which scenario playbooks to build by plotting likely crises on **likelihood × impact**. Build full playbooks for the high/high quadrant first; lighter templates suffice for low-likelihood events. This keeps the plan usable rather than encyclopedic.

## Tabletop simulations

If the user wants to *test* a plan, design a simulation: pick a realistic scenario, inject events in real time ("a journalist just called," "the number turned out to be 10x worse," "an employee tweeted"), and observe where decisions stall, messages contradict, or roles are unclear. Debrief against detection-to-response time and message consistency. Output: a list of fixes folded back into the plan.
