# Crisis Communication Management

A skill for managing business communications during high-stakes crises, built for communications and PR professionals and crisis response teams. It encodes the operating model, decision frameworks, and output templates that separate a controlled response from a panicked one.

## The core tension

Every crisis communication decision sits between two opposing failure modes: **moving too slowly** (a vacuum fills with rumor, speculation, and adversarial framing) and **moving too fast** (you say something false, premature, or legally damaging that you cannot retract). The job is not to pick a side but to manage the trade-off deliberately at every step — buying time with honest holding messages while verification catches up, and never letting accuracy be the casualty of speed.

Three principles govern everything below:

1. **Single source of truth.** All external and internal messages must trace to one verified, version-controlled fact base. Contradiction between two company spokespeople is itself a second crisis.
2. **Lead with the stakeholders most harmed, not the most powerful.** Affected customers, employees, and the public come before convenience to the business. Audiences talk to each other; sequencing that looks self-serving leaks and compounds the damage.
3. **You own the narrative or it owns you.** Acknowledge, don't speculate; correct fast; never lie or stonewall. Trust lost to a cover-up is harder to rebuild than trust lost to the original event.

## Phase 0 — Triage and severity classification

Before writing a single word, classify the crisis. Severity drives who is activated, how fast you respond, and how high it escalates. Score on these axes:

- **Stakeholder harm** — is anyone physically hurt, financially exposed, or having data compromised? Harm to people outranks harm to the brand, always.
- **Spread and velocity** — is it contained or already trending? Local or national/global? Slow-burn or breaking in real time?
- **Attribution** — is the organization seen as the victim, as having had an accident, or as having done something preventable/intentional? (This determines response strategy in Phase 2.)
- **Regulatory / legal exposure** — does this trigger mandatory disclosure, reporting clocks, or litigation risk?
- **Duration** — one-news-cycle event or a structural problem that will recur?

Translate the scores into a level:

- **Level 1 (Localized):** limited audience, low harm, no legal trigger. Handled by the comms lead with a prepared response.
- **Level 2 (Significant):** real harm or media interest, possible regulatory angle. Activate the core crisis team; brief leadership.
- **Level 3 (Critical):** serious harm, fast spread, legal/regulatory clocks running, executive or board involvement required. Full activation; CEO may be the face.

State the level explicitly to the user and let it drive your recommendations. Re-triage as facts change — crises escalate and de-escalate.

## Phase 1 — The golden hour (first response)

The opening window sets the tone for everything after. Two things happen in parallel: **stand up the response** and **fill the vacuum** with a holding statement.

Stand up the response:
- Convene the crisis team and name a single **incident commander** and a single **spokesperson**. Everyone else routes inquiries to them.
- Open the **fact log** (the single source of truth): what is confirmed, what is suspected, what is unknown, timestamped, with owners for each open question.
- Instruct all staff: do not speculate, do not comment, route everything to comms. One careless DM becomes the story.

Fill the vacuum with a **holding statement** — a short, honest message issued fast, before full facts are known. Its job is to show you are aware, you care, and you are acting — not to explain everything. A good holding statement:
- Acknowledges the situation and expresses appropriate concern for those affected.
- States what you are doing (investigating, cooperating, working to fix it).
- Avoids speculation on cause, blame, or numbers you cannot stand behind.
- Says when you will say more, and then meets that commitment.

Never let "no comment" be the message — it reads as guilt. "We are aware and actively investigating; we will share verified information as soon as we have it" is almost always available and almost always better. See `references/statements.md` for the full anatomy, templates, and the line-by-line do/don't.

## Phase 2 — Strategy selection (match response to crisis type)

Not every crisis is answered the same way. The response posture must match how much responsibility the organization bears — this is the central insight of Situational Crisis Communication Theory (SCCT). Misjudging it is the most common strategic error: a defensive denial when you're clearly at fault destroys trust, and an over-apology when you're the victim invites blame you didn't earn.

Map the crisis to a cluster, then to a posture:

- **Victim cluster** (natural disaster, sabotage, false rumor, product tampering by an outsider): the organization is itself harmed. Posture is primarily **inform and reassure**; correct falsehoods firmly; show you are protecting stakeholders.
- **Accidental cluster** (technical-error accident, equipment failure, unforeseen failure where intent is absent): moderate responsibility. Posture is **diminish** — explain context honestly without excusing — combined with concrete corrective action.
- **Preventable cluster** (human-error accidents, misconduct, knowingly violated rules, mismanagement): high responsibility. Posture is **rebuild** — full apology, acceptance of responsibility, remediation, and compensation where appropriate. Anything less reads as evasion.

Layer **bolstering** (reminding stakeholders of your track record, prior good acts) only as a secondary move, and never as a substitute for accountability in a preventable crisis. `references/crisis-typology.md` maps common business crises to clusters and gives the tactical playbook for each family.

## Phase 3 — Stakeholder sequencing and channels

Decide who hears what, when, and how. Build a stakeholder map ranked by harm and influence, then sequence outreach so no high-priority audience learns about the crisis from the press. Typical priority order: people directly harmed → employees → regulators/partners with obligations → customers broadly → media/public → investors (timed to legal requirements).

Match each audience to a channel and a tailored message drawn from one shared message house, so the facts are consistent but the framing fits the audience. Establish a **single owned channel** (status page, dedicated web page, pinned post) as the authoritative source you point everyone back to. See `references/stakeholders.md` for the mapping matrix, escalation protocol, RACI, and channel selection logic.

## Phase 4 — Sustained response

Crises rarely resolve in one statement. Set a **cadence** of updates (even "no new information" updates maintain trust), monitor coverage and sentiment, log every emerging fact into the single source of truth, and correct misinformation quickly and specifically. Watch for the **second-day story** — the follow-on angle about your response itself ("the cover-up is worse than the crime"). Keep the spokesperson on-message using the message house; brief them before every interaction (`references/internal-comms.md` covers spokesperson prep).

## Phase 5 — Recovery and post-crisis review

When acute pressure passes, shift from defense to rebuilding: demonstrate the changes made, follow through visibly on commitments, and tell the longer story of remediation. Then run an honest **post-incident review**: what was the detection-to-response time, where did messaging lag or contradict, what worked, what gets baked into the playbook. The deliverable is an updated plan, not blame.

## Output standards

Whatever the deliverable, hold to these:
- **Accuracy over polish.** Never invent facts, numbers, names, or timelines. Where the user hasn't supplied a fact, leave a clearly marked placeholder like `[CONFIRMED FIGURE]` rather than guessing — a fabricated detail in a crisis statement is a catastrophe.
- **Plain language.** Short sentences, no jargon, no euphemism. "We were hacked and your data may be affected," not "a third-party threat actor accessed certain environments."
- **Empathy before logistics.** Acknowledge human impact before explaining process.
- **Legal-aware, not legalese.** Flag where wording carries liability and recommend legal review, but don't bury the human message in defensive language. When financial, legal, or regulatory exposure is in play, note that you are not a lawyer and that counsel must review before release.
- **Always offer the next artifact.** A holding statement implies a follow-up; a plan implies a simulation. Point the user to the logical next step.
